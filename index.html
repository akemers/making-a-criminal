Making a Criminal

Are immigration authorities manufacturing criminal charges to justify deportations?

Doesn't fit the profile

After Jose Isaias Lara-Serrano was detained by immigration agents on his way to work, the New Orleans Congress of Day Laborers organized for his release, arguing he didn’t fit any Department of Homeland Security priorities. But according his advocates, before Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) released him, immigration officials worked with the U.S. Attorney for the Eastern District of Louisiana to charge him with a felony based solely on his prior deportations.

Criminalizing Immigrants

Lara-Serrano is one of thousands in the U.S. facing criminal prosecution on top of eventual deportation for “illegal re-entry” without any other criminal record. His case sheds light on what immigrant rights activists say is the larger problem: How ICE is criminalizing immigrants and punishing them with hefty federal charges and prison time, and then categorizing them as “priority” for deportation.

Even when data show that deportations* have decreased federal criminal prosecutions of immigrants across the U.S. have steadily increased. There is a significant increase in 2008, when President Obama was elected.

Why New Orleans?

New Orleans has experienced an infrastructural, economic, and cultural upheaval in the last decade. The city has seen a spike in the Latino immigrant population since Hurricane Katrina in 2005. Day laborers traveled to New Orleans looking for work and helped rebuild the city. Lara-Serrano arrived after the storm and found work in the construction industry.

Facing prosecution

Local Trends

Lara-Serrano is being prosecuted in New Orleans, Louisiana, a state divided into three judicial districts that manage all criminal prosecutions. When combined, it is clear the growth of charges for re-entry follow the national trend.

In addition to Hurricane Katrina, another key point includes the start of the Criminal Alien Removal Initiative (CARI), a pilot program by the New Orleans ICE office that used biometric fingerprinting machines to find immigrants with prior immigration violations or criminal records. Local immigrant rights activists note for example that between 2012 and 2013, when the operation was in progress, reentry prosecutions went down and when the program ended they began to increase again.

What Now

This information has raised important questions for Lara-Serrano’s advocates and others working with immigrants facing reentry prosecutions:

  • Is the increase of reentry prosecutions a strategy to label immigrants as “criminals”—even when they have no other criminal record—and supplement the number of deportations that fit their “priority” criteria?
  • And, in New Orleans, how much are these prosecutions fueled by anti-Latino racism and a response to local immigration policies, rather than an interest in immigration enforcement and public safety?

As Lara-Serrano goes to court on June 9, 2016, immigrant rights advocates at the New Orleans Congress of Day Laborers are looking to meet with the U.S. attorney to raise these questions and to ask for his charges to be dropped.

* By number of deportations we mean the sum of the number of removal cases plus the number of voluntary departure cases.

Removal: The expulsion of an alien from the United States. This expulsion may be based on grounds of inadmissibility or deportability.

Voluntary Departure: The departure of an alien from the United States without an order of removal. The departure may or may not have been preceded by a hearing before an immigration judge.

An alien allowed to voluntarily depart concedes removability but does not have a bar to seeking admission at a port-of-entry at any time. Failure to depart within the time granted results in a fine and a ten-year bar to several forms of relief from deportation. Definitions taken from the US Department of Homeland Security
